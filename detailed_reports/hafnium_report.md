## US and Allies Domestic Current Production (tons)  
• **France (EU)** – Orano’s La Hague zirconium plant (Manche, France) is the world’s leading hafnium producer, yielding roughly 30–35 t Hf/yr (≈43% of global output) ([www.ege.fr](https://www.ege.fr/infoguerre/hafnium-la-preeminence-francaise-menacee#:~:text=premier%20plan%20dans%20la%20production,de%20la%20production)) ([www.argusmedia.com](https://www.argusmedia.com/ja/news-and-insights/latest-market-news/2677094-hafnium-supply-must-expand-to-meet-demand-mmta#:~:text=Meanwhile%2C%20France%27s%20leading%20producer%20announced,metal%20next%20year%2C%20Argus%20understands)). It refines zirconium intermediates (from imported heavy-mineral sand concentrates) into nuclear-grade Zr–Hf alloys and separates out Hf.  
• **United States** – The US has **no primary hafnium mines** (domestic mined output = 0 t/yr). Two specialty chemical firms (in Oregon and Utah) separate hafnium from imported zirconium feedstock to produce pure Hf metal. These facilities currently operate below capacity, relying on foreign zirconium chemicals; USGS notes the US “produces hafnium but is not self-sufficient” ([www.argusmedia.com](https://www.argusmedia.com/en/news-and-insights/latest-market-news/2675178-fresh-concerns-over-the-us-80pc-tariff-on-hafnium#:~:text=The%20US%20produces%20hafnium%20but,on%20imports%20to%20meet%20demand)). They could potentially add ∼50 t more (combined) if expanded ([www.argusmedia.com](https://www.argusmedia.com/ja/news-and-insights/latest-market-news/2677094-hafnium-supply-must-expand-to-meet-demand-mmta#:~:text=In%20the%20US%2C%20hafnium%20metal,existing%20permits%2C%20according%20to%20Matheson)), but actual reported output data are not published.  
• **Canada** – Rio Tinto’s Fer et Titane (Quebec) mines heavy-mineral sands (ilmenite, rutile, zircon), but it produces titanium pigment and iron, not refined hafnium. Canada does not refine Hf metal domestically. However, Canada has been a significant supplier of zircon concentrates and semi-processed zirconium: historically ~22% of US hafnium imports came via Canadian sources ([industrial-supply-un.blogspot.com](https://industrial-supply-un.blogspot.com/2009/04/hafniumzirconium-production.html#:~:text=Japan%2C%207,and%20other%2C%202)). Hafnium remains locked in the zircon byproduct.  
• **Australia** – Major mineral-sands miners (Iluka, Tronox and others in WA, NSW, QLD) produce hundreds of thousands of tonnes of zircon concentrate annually (e.g. Jacinth-Ambrosia mine ≈215 kt Zr concentrate in 2024). These concentrates contain ~1–1.5% HfO₂, but **no domestic Hf refining**. Almost all zircon is shipped abroad, and associated Hf is separated overseas. Heavy-mineral deposits (coastal placer sands) are the modal source of zircon worldwide ([pubs.usgs.gov](https://pubs.usgs.gov/publication/pp1802V#:~:text=zirconium,percent%20of%20the%20world%E2%80%99s%20zircon)) ([world-nuclear.org](https://world-nuclear.org/information-library/appendices/mineral-sands-appendix-to-norm-information-paper#:~:text=some%20manganese%20and%20magnesium%3B%20and,traces%20of%20uranium%20and%20thorium)).  
• **Japan (and other allies)** – Japan has no hafnium mines; its small metallurgy industry processes imported zirconium intermediates. Japanese firms have been major consumers (especially for nuclear control rods) but not producers. Likewise, other allies (Norway, Iceland) have no hafnium mining or processing. (Japan has increased Hf purchases along with S.Korea to support reactor expansions ([www.argusmedia.com](https://www.argusmedia.com/en/news-and-insights/latest-market-news/2675178-fresh-concerns-over-the-us-80pc-tariff-on-hafnium#:~:text=Global%20competition%20for%20hafnium%20is,is%20used%20in%20nuclear%20rods)).)  

## US and Allies Domestic Demand 2030 (tons)  
Allied hafnium demand in 2030 will be driven by advanced technology sectors. Key projected demands:  
• **Semiconductor industry** – HfO₂ is used as a high-κ gate dielectric and dielectric barrier in memory (e.g. DRAM/flash) and logic chips. Matheson (Nanoscale Powders) projects semiconductor-related Hf demand to reach ~64 t/yr by 2030, up from ~40 t/yr in 2024 ([www.argusmedia.com](https://www.argusmedia.com/en/news-and-insights/latest-market-news/2678155-semiconductors-alter-minor-metal-demand-supply-balances#:~:text=Semiconductor%20demand%20for%20hafnium%20could,nuclear%20uses%20to%2018%20t%2Fyr)). Much of this demand comes from allied producers (US fabs, Taiwanese/Japanese foundries supplying global markets).  
• **Aerospace & superalloys** – Hafnium is a key additive to Ni-based superalloys (for turbine blades in aircraft/space engines) and high-temperature materials. Demand for Hf in superalloys is forecast to reach ~60 t/yr by 2030 ([www.argusmedia.com](https://www.argusmedia.com/en/news-and-insights/latest-market-news/2678155-semiconductors-alter-minor-metal-demand-supply-balances#:~:text=Semiconductor%20demand%20for%20hafnium%20could,nuclear%20uses%20to%2018%20t%2Fyr)). Allied aerospace industries (US, EU, Japan) will consume a substantial share.  
• **Nuclear energy** – Hafnium’s neutron-absorbing properties make it useful in control rods (especially in military/naval reactors). Projected nuclear-sector Hf demand is ~18 t/yr by 2030 ([www.argusmedia.com](https://www.argusmedia.com/en/news-and-insights/latest-market-news/2678155-semiconductors-alter-minor-metal-demand-supply-balances#:~:text=Semiconductor%20demand%20for%20hafnium%20could,nuclear%20uses%20to%2018%20t%2Fyr)). Rising reactor construction in allied countries (France, US, Japan) will sustain this demand.  
• **Other applications** – Hafnium is used in specialty electronics (plasma cutting tips, catalytic converters, optics) and emerging alloys. While smaller (<5 t/yr), these uses will grow with allied high-tech manufacturing.  
• **Combined allied demand** – Given a global forecast of ~180 t by 2030 ([www.argusmedia.com](https://www.argusmedia.com/ja/news-and-insights/latest-market-news/2677094-hafnium-supply-must-expand-to-meet-demand-mmta#:~:text=Global%20hafnium%20demand%20could%20increase,rods%2C%20space%20alloys%20and%20semiconductors)), US and allied nations (US, Canada, EU, Japan, etc.) likely account for the majority (~70–100 t) of this demand. (For example, DOE and DoD requirements in aerospace/nuclear, combined with European reactor and chip-sector needs, will drive tens of tonnes of aggregate Hf consumption.)  

## US and Allies Domestic Supply 2030 (tons)  
Assuming planned expansions and optimizations, US/allied Hf supply could increase markedly by 2030:  
• **France** – Orano (La Hague) announced scaling hafnium output. Current metal production (~30 t/yr) is slated to rise to ~45 t/yr within a year ([www.argusmedia.com](https://www.argusmedia.com/ja/news-and-insights/latest-market-news/2677094-hafnium-supply-must-expand-to-meet-demand-mmta#:~:text=Meanwhile%2C%20France%27s%20leading%20producer%20announced,metal%20next%20year%2C%20Argus%20understands)), as new nuclear fuel alloy capacity comes online. This adds ~15 t/yr to allied supply.  
• **United States** – The two U.S. zirconium processors (Pacific Northwest) have expansion permits. Combined they could add ≈50 t/yr of Hf (based on permitted reactor/plant capacity) once justified ([www.argusmedia.com](https://www.argusmedia.com/ja/news-and-insights/latest-market-news/2677094-hafnium-supply-must-expand-to-meet-demand-mmta#:~:text=In%20the%20US%2C%20hafnium%20metal,existing%20permits%2C%20according%20to%20Matheson)). Realizing this requires substantial investment and feedstock (imported Zr intermediates).  
• **Secondary/Refinery expansions** – Several rhenium/molybdenum refineries (in France, US) are reconfiguring to produce hafnium. Matheson noted existing symmetrical facilities could “meet the anticipated rise in demand with significant expansions” ([www.argusmedia.com](https://www.argusmedia.com/ja/news-and-insights/latest-market-news/2677094-hafnium-supply-must-expand-to-meet-demand-mmta#:~:text=Global%20supply%20of%20pure%20hafnium,over%20the%20next%20few%20years)). If realized, even dozens of additional tonnes could be available.  
• **Recycling and re-­capture** – Improved recycling (recycling Hf from superalloy scrap or electronic waste) and dehafniation of existing zirconium plants (recovering residual Hf from Zr chemical streams) could supply a few tonnes annually ([www.argusmedia.com](https://www.argusmedia.com/ja/news-and-insights/latest-market-news/2677094-hafnium-supply-must-expand-to-meet-demand-mmta#:~:text=On%20the%20bright%20side%2C%20improving,help%20alleviate%20the%20supply%20imbalance)). Allied programs (e.g. DOE/R&D) are targeting Hf recovery from spent reactor fuel and industrial scrap.  
• **New mining projects** – No major new hafnium-specific mines are known in allied economies. Some allied mineral-sand projects (Australia, Canada) are evaluating zircon output expansion, but these mainly feed foreign refiners. Thus, allied supply growth relies on upgrading refining capacity and recycling, not new ores.  
Overall, if expansions proceed, US/allied hafnium output by 2030 might reach on the order of 80–100 t/yr (e.g. France ≈45 t + US ≈50 t), plus incremental recycled volumes, still shy of total demand.

## Key Discovery Technologies  
• **Radiometric (Gamma-Ray) Surveys**: Heavy-mineral sands containing zircon often include monazite (Th-bearing) or thorium-rich minerals ([world-nuclear.org](https://world-nuclear.org/information-library/appendices/mineral-sands-appendix-to-norm-information-paper#:~:text=some%20manganese%20and%20magnesium%3B%20and,traces%20of%20uranium%20and%20thorium)). Airborne gamma spectrometry (thorium channel) can thus highlight paleoplacer sands likely to carry zircon/hafnium. For example, northern Australia’s Zr–Ti sands were located in part via airborne geophysics.  
• **Geological Mapping & Geochemistry**: Identifying the correct host-rock is crucial. Geologists map ancient coastal/river paloplacers and carbonatite/pegmatite intrusions where zircon/baddeleyite may concentrate ([pubs.usgs.gov](https://pubs.usgs.gov/publication/pp1802V#:~:text=zirconium,percent%20of%20the%20world%E2%80%99s%20zircon)). Systematic sampling of stream sediments or soils can reveal elevated Zr and Hf anomalies. Geochemical analysis (ICP-MS) of heavy-mineral fractions from drill cores can directly detect zircon (hence Hf) grade.  
• **Remote Sensing & Geophysics**: Satellite imagery and lidar help delineate paleo-beach sands. Airborne magnetics/gravity may detect buried heavy-mineral anomalies. In some cases (e.g. Kola Peninsula), magnetic surveys aided discovery of baddeleyite (ZrO₂) in magnetite-apatite ores. While Hf itself is not radioactive, associated Th/U in zircon/monazite makes radiometry useful.  
• **Exploration Drilling**: Once targets are identified, drilling (rotary or cone) with dense media separation or downhole logging tests heavy-mineral content (high-density fraction) to confirm zircon/hafnium presence. Core samples undergoing heavy-liquid separation yield concentrate that is assayed for Zr and Hf oxides.  

## Key Extraction Technologies  
• **Open-pit & Dredge Mining**: Hafnium enters the supply chain as a component of zircon in heavy-mineral placer deposits. These are mined by open-pit or dredging methods using slurry extraction. For example, Australia’s Eneabba and Murray Basin mines use hydraulic dredges or draglines to strip marine sands. The mined sand is washed through **spiral concentrators** (gravity separation) that isolate heavy minerals (rutile, ilmenite, zircon) ([world-nuclear.org](https://world-nuclear.org/information-library/appendices/mineral-sands-appendix-to-norm-information-paper#:~:text=These%20mineral%20sands%20are%20in,for%20ilmenite)).  
• **Wet Concentration**: Spiral sluices and multi-stage heavy-media cyclones concentrate heavy-mineral sands on-site. Ilmenite (magnetic) is removed by magnetic separators, leaving a non-magnetic heavy sand rich in zircon (with Hf) and rutile ([world-nuclear.org](https://world-nuclear.org/information-library/appendices/mineral-sands-appendix-to-norm-information-paper#:~:text=mining%20plant%20they%20are%20concentrated,for%20ilmenite)).  
• **Dry Separation (if applicable)**: In some satellite or low-moisture operations, dense media separation (sink/float using ferrosilicon slurry) and high-intensity magnetic or electrostatic (eddy-current) separators may be used to segregate zircon/ilmenite fractions. These methods enhance zircon grade ahead of chemical processing.  

## Key Separation Technologies  
• **Solvent Extraction (Hydrometallurgy)**: The dominant method is multistage organic–aqueous extraction. Zirconium–hafnium ores (as chlorides or sulfates) are treated with organophosphorus reagents (e.g. tributyl phosphate, D2EHPA). Zr and Hf form chloride complexes that have slightly different affinities for the solvent. By serial extraction and stripping, hafnium is progressively removed from zirconium solutions. USGS literature notes current reactors use “multi-step hydrometallurgical liquid–liquid extraction for hafnium removal” ([www.researchgate.net](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/287457696_Separation_of_Zirconium_and_Hafnium_A_Review#:~:text=cracking%2C%20multi,Using)). This is the basis of the classic C-V process (H-intergroup separations).  
• **Extractive Distillation (Pyrometallurgy)**: Hafnium and zirconium tetrachlorides have different vapor pressures. Studies report HfCl₄ boils (condenses) around 523 K, while ZrCl₄ is less volatile ([www.researchgate.net](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/287457696_Separation_of_Zirconium_and_Hafnium_A_Review#:~:text=3)). By heating a melt of mixed Zr/Hf chlorides (or using a salt–distillation process), hafnium tetrachloride can be preferentially vaporized and separated. A commercial “chloride distillation” method has been used to concentrate Hf, exploiting this volatility difference ([www.researchgate.net](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/287457696_Separation_of_Zirconium_and_Hafnium_A_Review#:~:text=3)).  
• **Molten Salt / Pyrolysis Routes**: Innovative methods employ molten salt baths or high-temperature reduction. One approach dissolves Zr–Hf oxides in a NaK–Cl melt and electrolyses or distills the chlorides. Patents describe separating Hf by reductive distillation in a Na/K chloride eutectic ([patents.justia.com](https://patents.justia.com/patent/3966458#:~:text=US%20Patent%20for%20Separation%20of,Hafnium%20tetrachloride%20and%20zirconium%20tetrachloride)). Such pyrometallurgical (thermal, electrochemical) techniques can simplify flowsheets, though they are less common industrially.  

## Key Purification & Refinement Technologies  
• **Kroll Process Reduction**: Once hafnium tetrachloride is isolated, it is typically reduced with magnesium or calcium (mimicking the zirconium Kroll process). Mg reduces HfCl₄ to sponge metal. The raw Hf sponge contains residual Mg and salts, so it is subsequently leached and slagged. The sponge is then vacuum-arc or electron-beam melted into ingots ([www.researchgate.net](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/287457696_Separation_of_Zirconium_and_Hafnium_A_Review#:~:text=cracking%2C%20multi,Using)). (USGS notes zirconium production via Kroll; the same principles apply to hafnium due to their chemistry ([www.researchgate.net](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/287457696_Separation_of_Zirconium_and_Hafnium_A_Review#:~:text=cracking%2C%20multi,Using)).)  
• **Vacuum Arc Remelting / EBM**: To achieve nuclear-grade Hf, melting in a vacuum or inert gas (using consumable electrodes) removes inclusions and homogenizes the alloy. This also reduces gas and impurity content. Specialized furnaces (ER/VAR) are used by alloy suppliers (e.g. producer VSMPO-AVISMA for titanium alloys also handles Zr/Hf alloys).  
• **Zone Refining**: For ultra-high purity, techniques like multiple-zone melting can strip trace contaminants. Though less common for CMCs, some producers apply repeated melting/solidification to 99.99% purity.  
• **Alternate Processes**: Some R&D methods (Chloride Salt Electronics ([www.researchgate.net](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/287457696_Separation_of_Zirconium_and_Hafnium_A_Review#:~:text=processes%20are%20roughly%20classified%20into,The))) propose low-temperature electrorefinement of Zr/Hf from ionic melts. However, these are not yet commercial. In practice, the primary bulk of refined hafnium metal comes from chloride reduction and remelting.  

## Key Remediation Technologies  
• **Monazite/Thorium Management**: Heavy-mineral sands often contain monazite (5–10% Th) ([world-nuclear.org](https://world-nuclear.org/information-library/appendices/mineral-sands-appendix-to-norm-information-paper#:~:text=some%20manganese%20and%20magnesium%3B%20and,traces%20of%20uranium%20and%20thorium)), which must be handled safely. Industry practice is to process out rare-earths and then blend residual monazite back into the tailings. For example, Western Australia’s mineral-sand mines historically shipped ∼160 kt of monazite to France, but ceased when disposal of radioactive waste became problematic ([world-nuclear.org](https://world-nuclear.org/information-library/appendices/mineral-sands-appendix-to-norm-information-paper#:~:text=Monazite%20and%20xenotime%20may%20be,and%20dispersed%20with%20the%20tailings)). Now monazite is returned to waste beds under cover, mitigating radiation.  
• **Tailings Dewatering & Dry Stacking**: After wet concentration, fines are thickened and often “dry stacked” (consolidated) to minimize leaching. Technologies include vacuum belt filtration and cyclone thickeners. Dry stacking reduces ponded water and dust; WNA notes that seawater/groundwater in sand mines is recycled, and tailings are reclaimed (e.g. re-vegetation of dredged pits) to restore ecosystems ([world-nuclear.org](https://world-nuclear.org/information-library/appendices/mineral-sands-appendix-to-norm-information-paper#:~:text=Monazite%20and%20xenotime%20may%20be,and%20dispersed%20with%20the%20tailings)).  
• **Dust Suppression & Air Controls**: In processing plants, wet scrubbers and enclosure of conveyors/slurries prevent silica and metal-laden dust. If any Zn/Hf refractory fines are present, filtration systems capture them.  
• **Water Treatment**: Effluents (from washing and leach circuits) are treated to precipitate metals and neutralize Acids. Water recovery systems (reverse osmosis, ion exchange) ensure recycled water meets environmental discharge standards. These measures, standard in allied heavy-sand operations, limit heavy-metal spread.

## Key Bottleneck technology for US and Allies Domestic Production  
• **Zr–Hf Separation** – The foremost bottleneck is the complex separation of hafnium from chemically near-identical zirconium. Existing solvent-extraction and distillation processes are multi-step and capital intensive. Argus reports specifically that a “limited number of producers and technological bottlenecks in the separation of hafnium and zirconium” have created a supply shortage ([www.argusmedia.com](https://www.argusmedia.com/ja/news-and-insights/latest-market-news/2486868-output-cuts-tight-feedstock-to-rein-in-china-s-hafnium#:~:text=A%20shortage%20in%20oxide%20feedstock,affected%20many%20metal%20producers%27%20output)). Improving extractant chemistry or pyro-processing is critical, but no breakthrough has yet radically lowered cost or cycle time.  
• **Feedstock Availability** – The US/allies lack significant domestic zirconium ore. All hafnium production depends on imported zirconium-rich concentrates. Consequently, allied hafnium plants are highly sensitive to zircon feed availability. Without new mines in allied territories, this remains a supply constraint.  
• **Regulatory & Trade Restrictions** – Stringent export controls and tariffs add friction. For instance, recent U.S. tariffs have pushed Chinese hafnium to 79% duties ([www.argusmedia.com](https://www.argusmedia.com/en/news-and-insights/latest-market-news/2675178-fresh-concerns-over-the-us-80pc-tariff-on-hafnium#:~:text=The%20new%2034pc%20reciprocal%20tariff,Trump%20took%20office%20in%20January)), and France imposed 20% on Hf to the U.S. This curbs alternative supply. Meanwhile, permitting delays for plant expansions (noted by Argus) impede quick scale-up ([www.argusmedia.com](https://www.argusmedia.com/ja/news-and-insights/latest-market-news/2677094-hafnium-supply-must-expand-to-meet-demand-mmta#:~:text=There%20are%20several%20factors%20which,challenges%20in%20obtaining%20specialty%20materials)). Skilled labor and specialized equipment shortages (vacuum systems, nickel crucibles) also slow capacity additions.  

## Key Bottleneck summary  
The critical technology bottleneck is the **hafnium–zirconium separation process**, which remains technologically demanding due to the elements’ chemical similarity ([www.argusmedia.com](https://www.argusmedia.com/ja/news-and-insights/latest-market-news/2486868-output-cuts-tight-feedstock-to-rein-in-china-s-hafnium#:~:text=A%20shortage%20in%20oxide%20feedstock,affected%20many%20metal%20producers%27%20output)) ([www.researchgate.net](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/287457696_Separation_of_Zirconium_and_Hafnium_A_Review#:~:text=cracking%2C%20multi,Using)). Allied production must import zircon feedstock (since US mines none) and relies on a handful of specialty refineries; any feed or refinery disruption immediately constrains Hf output. Compounding this, high tariffs and export controls (e.g. the US 79% duty on Chinese Hf ([www.argusmedia.com](https://www.argusmedia.com/en/news-and-insights/latest-market-news/2675178-fresh-concerns-over-the-us-80pc-tariff-on-hafnium#:~:text=The%20new%2034pc%20reciprocal%20tariff,Trump%20took%20office%20in%20January))) and lengthy permitting inhibit rapid deployment of new separation/refining capacity. In sum, without breakthroughs in Zr–Hf separation or major new zircon sources, allied hafnium supply will struggle to match rising demand ([www.argusmedia.com](https://www.argusmedia.com/ja/news-and-insights/latest-market-news/2486868-output-cuts-tight-feedstock-to-rein-in-china-s-hafnium#:~:text=A%20shortage%20in%20oxide%20feedstock,affected%20many%20metal%20producers%27%20output)) ([www.argusmedia.com](https://www.argusmedia.com/ja/news-and-insights/latest-market-news/2677094-hafnium-supply-must-expand-to-meet-demand-mmta#:~:text=separation%20plants%20to%20recover%20additional,help%20alleviate%20the%20supply%20imbalance)).
